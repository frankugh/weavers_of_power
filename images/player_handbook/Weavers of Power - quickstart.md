# Weavers of Power — Quickstart (v0.5)

{{wide
### *A card-driven tabletop RPG where players sometimes write the story themselves*
![weavers_of_power_header](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/frankugh/weavers_of_power/main/images/player_handbook/quickstart_p1.png) {width:672px}

Every character in Weavers of Power is a wielder of mystical abilities. You will weave the very energies that shape reality itself: 
the **martial** power of blade and body, the **elemental** fury of storm 
and fire, the **light** of healing and protection, the wild pulse of 
**nature**, and the cold, dark reach of **shadow**. You build a deck. 
You draw cards each turn. You spend energy to strike, defend, and cast.

Although **Weavecraft** takes many forms — martial, elemental, light, 
nature, shadow — the true source of a weaver's strength is **willpower**. You carry a 
pool of willpower to spend across the day. During skill checks, you 
draw cards. Some show a **fate** symbol. When you spend willpower to 
claim them, the GM steps aside — and you narrate what happens next.

You introduce the stranger in the tavern. The hidden door. The stroke 
of impossible luck. You shape the world, with the GM as your editor.

Quick setup. Tactical combat. Light deckbuilding. And every now and 
then, the storyteller's chair.

##### **This quickstart contains:**

- Everything you need to play your first session
- Six ready-made characters, using all five energy types
- *The Lantern at Hollow Crossing* — a complete starter scenario
- A one-page reference card for the table

Designed for **3–5 players + one Game Master**. First sessions typically run 2–3 hours.

# Setting Up Your First Session

## What you'll need

**Printed materials** *(downloadable PDFs at [link])*:

- This quickstart document
- Six character sheets — one per player
- Six character decks — one per player
- The NPC card packs — for the GM to use during the scenario
- Printable willpower tokens

**At the table:**

- Pens or pencils
- Scratch paper for notes
- *Optional but helpful:* a battlemap or grid, plus miniatures or tokens for positioning during combat

That's it. No dice. Everything is decided by the cards.

{{note
##### Tip
Print character decks on heavier cardstock, or sleeve them with blank cards from a hobby store. The cards get shuffled often. For willpower tokens, printed counters work fine — but coins, beads, or glass stones do the job just as well.
}}

## Roles at the table

**One Game Master.** Runs the world, plays the NPCs, makes rulings, and narrates what the players' characters experience. Reads the scenario in advance and prepares to be flexible.

**Three to five players.** Each takes one of the pre-made characters and plays them for the session. You don't need to read the whole document beforehand — your character sheet will tell you most of what you need to know.

## Time

A first session typically runs **2–3 hours** of play, plus 15–30 minutes of setup the first time. Once your group has played together once, setup drops to about 5 minutes.

The starter scenario fits one session comfortably. If your group is enjoying the moment-to-moment play and runs out of time, there's a natural breakpoint partway through Act 3 — pause there and resume next time.

## Before play, the GM should…

- **Read the whole quickstart at least once.** You need to know where the scenario goes, even if you improvise within it.
- **Review the six pre-made characters.** Knowing what each player can do helps you frame challenges that give everyone a moment to shine.
- **Print all the materials, including spares.** Cards and tokens have a way of getting lost.
- **Skim the GM Notes throughout this document.** They flag the moments where the game asks something specific from you.

{{note
##### GM Note
The most important prep isn't memorizing rules — it's reading the scenario and feeling out the rhythm. Players forgive a GM who pauses to look something up. They struggle with a GM who doesn't know what's around the next bend.
}}

## Before play, players should…

- **Pick a character.** Six pre-made characters are available; pick any combination that appeals to your group. The Fighter is direct and durable, the Wizard versatile and reactive, the Cleric supportive and resilient, the Shadowmaster fast and lethal, the Druid wild and adaptive, the Dark Mage cold and calculating.
- **Read your character sheet front to back.** It tells you your abilities, your deck, your signature moves, and a short backstory hook.
- **Bring your character to life.** A name, a voice, a quirk — anything that makes them yours.

That's it. Time to play.

# Skill Checks

When you try something difficult — picking a lock, persuading a guard, leaping a chasm — the GM may call for a **skill check**.

## How a check works

1. The GM tells you the **check type** — usually one of your six abilities, or a specialization if your character has one that fits.
2. You **draw cards** from your deck equal to your score in that ability. Each card shows one of three results in its skill check portion:

<div style="
  width:310px;
  height:130px;
  margin:0 auto;
  background-image:url('https://raw.githubusercontent.com/frankugh/weavers_of_power/main/images/Playing_Cards/explainer_skill_signs.png');
  background-size:310px auto;
  background-position:center center;
  background-repeat:no-repeat;
">
</div>

- ✓ **Success** — counts toward the check
- ◇ **Fate** — does nothing on its own, but…
- ✗ **Fail** — no help, sometimes a complication

3. If you drew one or more **fate** cards, you may spend 1 willpower to convert *all* of them into successes.
4. Tell the GM your total successes. They tell you what happens.

## The six abilities

Most skill checks use one of these:

- **Intelligence** — reasoning, recall, language, problem-solving
- **Alertness** — noticing, sensing danger, spotting details
- **Stealth** — hiding, moving silently
- **Social** — persuasion, intimidation, lying, reading people
- **Arcana** — knowledge of magic, lore of the weave, recognizing strange phenomena
- **Athletics** — running, climbing, jumping, swimming, brute force

Your character sheet lists your score in each. The higher the score, the more cards you draw on a check — and the better your odds.

## Spending willpower

Each character has a **willpower** pool — typically 5 points at session start. Spending 1 willpower on a check converts every fate card you drew into a success.

When you win a check by spending willpower, you may claim the **narration**: instead of the GM describing what happens, *you do*. This narration rule applies only to skill checks, not to hit draws in combat. How freely you can shape the moment depends on how strong your check turned out:

- **3+ successes** — your narration goes through clean. The GM rarely overrides; you have free rein within established facts.
- **1–2 successes** — the GM may still grant narration, but often with light guardrails. They might tweak a detail, narrow a claim, or add a wrinkle.

You don't break established facts. But you can introduce something new — a rope you happened to bring, a merchant who knew your name, a fortunate gust of wind — and the GM weaves it in.

This is one of the most distinctive things about Weavers of Power. Reach for the reins.

{{note
##### Tip
If you're unsure whether spending willpower is worth it, you can ask the GM in advance whether your check would earn a narration.
}}

## Example

> Mira tries to convince a suspicious gate guard to let her pass. The GM calls for a **Social check, target 2 successes**. Mira's Social score is 2, so she draws two cards: 1 Success, 1 Fate.
>
> *"I'll spend a willpower to turn that Fate into a Success — and I want the narration."*
>
> Mira now has 2 successes — enough to win, but only just. The GM offers narration with a light guardrail: *"You can shape this, but the guard won't simply wave you through — give me a reason he hesitates."*
>
> Mira leans in: *"My mother's pendant catches the lantern-light. The guard pales. 'You… you're old Tevin's daughter, aren't you?' He hesitates, hand on his blade — and steps aside."*
>
> The GM accepts it. Mira's father is now someone who once mattered to this guard. The door is open. The campaign just gained a thread.

{{note
##### GM Note — On giving up the reins
Let's be honest: handing narrations to players doesn't always make a GM's life easier. You lose a bit of control. You have to think on your feet. You sometimes get a curveball you weren't ready for.

But — it works. A player's mindset often shifts the moment narration is on the table: from *"let's see what we can break"* to *"we're building this story together."* The creativity that comes out of shared storytelling is remarkable. 

Lean into it. Yes-and where you can. Guardrail where you must. And players will surprise you with continuously making the world richer. 
}}

# Combat & Draws of Power

When swords come out — or when you weave powerful magic — the rules tighten. Combat in Weavers of Power runs in turns, and each turn opens with a **Draw of Power**: the cards that determine what energy you can wield this round.

## Initiative

The GM tracks turn order based on **Alertness**. More alert characters act before less alert ones — no roll required. The GM may shift enemy initiative slightly each round to keep things tense.

## Your turn, in order

**1. Draw of Power.** Draw cards from your deck until your hand contains a number of cards equal to your **effective Power level**. Wounds in your hand count against the draw limit. If your effective Power is 4 and you hold 2 Wounds, you draw only 2 fresh cards.

![card_explainer](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/frankugh/weavers_of_power/main/images/Playing_Cards/example_draw.png) {width:300px}

**2. Move and act.** You have **movement** and **2 actions**. You may move before, between, or after your actions, and break up movement freely.

**3. Free actions.** Your **Class card** and **Ancestry card** can be played at any point during your turn, free of action cost. Re-equipping items is also free.

**4. End of turn.** Discard all unused non-Wound cards. Wounds remain in hand.

{{note
##### What's in your hand?
Each card shows two things:

- An **energy type and value** — the resources you spend on actions this turn
- A **skill result** — relevant only if you use the card for a hit draw later

}}

## Two kinds of action

**Energy actions** spend X energy of a specific type from cards in your hand to power an effect:

- *Martial Strike X* — make a Strike with X Martial energy. Damage = X + weapon's Base DMG. Unarmed: X DMG and disadvantage on the hit draw.
- *Elemental Strike X* — attack at range with X Elemental energy. Damage = X + an Elemental weapon's Base DMG, or X DMG without one.
- *Heal X* — remove X Wounds from an ally's hand, one Wound per energy point.

**Non-energy actions** cost an action but no energy:

- *Dash* — double your movement this turn
- *Disengage* — your movement doesn't provoke opportunity attacks
- *Hide* — attempt to become hidden; requires a Stealth check
- *Shed* — discard 1 Wound card from your hand
- *Prepare* — draw 1 more card on your next Draw of Power

A full action menu is on the player mat — keep it in front of you for your first session.

**Opportunity attacks.** If you move out of an enemy's melee reach without using Disengage, that enemy may immediately make one basic melee attack against you. A creature can make at most one opportunity attack per round.
  
![card_explainer](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/frankugh/weavers_of_power/main/images/Playing_Cards/explainer_cards.png) {width:300px}

## Resolving an attack

When you make an offensive action — Strike, Elemental Strike, Dark Strike, Sickness, Vines, or a modified strike such as Burn — you make a **hit draw**:

1. Draw **3 cards** from your deck. These are fresh draws from the deck — not from your hand — read for skill result, then discarded.
2. Count your **Successes**.
\page

| Successes | Result |
|:---:|---|
| 0 | Miss — no effect |
| 1+ | Hit — apply Base value |
| 2+ | Apply weapon/ability bonus, if any |
| 3 | Critical — double damage on attacks |

Wounds drawn during a hit draw count as **Fail**.

You can spend **1 Willpower** to convert all Fate cards drawn into Successes — same as on a skill check.


## Defensive and supportive actions

Guard, Heal, Strengthen, Channel, and other defensive/supportive actions **automatically succeed** when cast. You spend the energy, the effect happens — no hit draw needed.

## Advantage and disadvantage

Some situations grant **advantage** (draw +1 hit card) or **disadvantage** (draw −1 hit card). They don't stack, and they cancel each other out.

If you draw more than 3 hit cards, count only the best 3 results for determining hits, bonuses, and criticals.

## A note on weapons

Some weapon combinations grant a small bonus on hit draws. Where this applies to your pre-made character, it has already been baked into their **Signature Actions** — you don't need to calculate anything.

*If you swap or pick up new weapons during play, check the Reference Guide for the full rule on Two-Weapon and Versatile bonuses.*

## Defenses and damage reduction

You have three layers of defense, in order. Damage works through them top to bottom:

**1. Armor** — flat damage reduction from worn protection. Mail armor gives 2 Armor; leather armor gives 1 Guard at start of turn instead (see below). Every point of Armor reduces incoming damage by 1, on every attack you take.

- **Magic Armor** works the same way, but comes from enchantments or spells rather than worn gear.
- **Stab** and **Pierce** weapons **ignore normal Armor**. Mira's daggers (Stab) cut through mail as if it weren't there.
- **Magic Armor cannot be ignored** by Stab or Pierce. It protects against everything.

**2. Guard** — a temporary damage pool that absorbs damage one-for-one until depleted. You gain Guard from:

- Your shield or light armor at the start of your turn (+1 Guard)
- Spending energy on a Guard action (X energy = X+2 Guard)
- Some spells and abilities

Guard resets to zero at the end of combat. It's protection you build *during* a fight.

**3. Toughness** — your durability stat. Once Armor and Guard are exhausted, damage reduces your Toughness pool. When Toughness reaches 0, you take a **Wound** (see next page).


{{note
##### Sample turn — Joren the Fighter

Joren starts his turn. His effective Power is 4, so he draws 4 cards from his deck:

- **M3** (Martial energy, value 3)
- **Master 1** (wildcard — can act as any energy type Joren has in his deck)
- **L1** (light energy, value 1)
- **Void** (no energy, only a skill result)

Joren wants a strong attack. He declares **Martial Strike 4**, using his M3 plus his Master card (which he treats as Martial 1 for this strike). Total Martial spent: 4.

Damage = X 4 + weapon Base DMG 3 = **7**. Joren draws 3 hit cards from his deck: Success, Fate, Success. That's 2 successes.

Joren spends 1 willpower to convert the Fate into a Success. Now **3 successes — a critical**. His longsword's 2+ bonus adds +1 DMG, then the critical doubles the total: (7 + 1) × 2 = **16**.

The L1 and Void cards stay in his hand, unused. At end of turn, he discards both. Next turn, he draws fresh to 4 again.
}}

- **Master Energy** (3) — wildcards. Act as any energy type in your deck.

# Wounds, Recovery, and Willpower

The combat rules are about what you do on your turn. The recovery rules are about what happens to you between turns — and they're where Weavers of Power's grit shows.

## When Toughness breaks: Wounds

Combat shows where Weavers of Power's grit lives. The damage reduction layers — Armor, Guard, Toughness — are explained on the previous page. This page is about what happens when Toughness gives way: **Wounds**.

When damage reduces Toughness to **0**, you take a **Wound**. Toughness then resets to full. Any excess damage from the same hit continues reducing Toughness from that reset value.

## What a Wound does

A Wound is a card. When you take one, it goes **directly into your hand** — it doesn't pass through your deck first.

Wounds do three things:

![card_explainer](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/frankugh/weavers_of_power/main/images/Playing_Cards/wound.png) {width:112px}

1. They **occupy a hand slot.** Your effective Power level is reduced by every Wound you hold.
2. They count as **Fail** on hit draws and skill checks.
3. They **stay** in your hand until properly removed. You do not discard Wounds at the end of your turn.

{{note
##### GM Note — On Wound pressure
Wounds are the rhythm-keeper of Weavers combat — but the danger comes in waves, not flatly. A fresh Wound sits in your hand: a slot is gone, your effective Power is reduced, and any hit draw or skill check it lands in fails. That's the sharp edge.

Once a player Heals it away, the Wound moves to the discard pile and eventually back into the deck — still there, still capable of biting on a future draw, but no longer choking the hand.

The system makes attrition feel like rising and falling tension. Two Wounds in hand is a character struggling. Two Wounds buried in their deck is a character carrying scars but still functional. *"Do we push on, or do we rest?"* becomes mechanically real — and the answer depends on where the Wounds currently are.
}}

## Removing Wounds

**The Shed action** *(short-term relief)* — as a non-energy action, discard 1 Wound card from your hand into your discard pile. The hand slot opens up, but the Wound cycles back through your deck and may return to your hand later. Useful in a fight; not real recovery.

**The Heal action** *(supportive relief)* — works similarly, but uses light energy and can remove multiple Wounds from an ally's hand, depending on how much energy is spent.

**Resting** *(recovery)* — rests clear Wounds out of your deck for a while, and long rests can remove some of them entirely.

**Short rest:** about 1 hour of downtime activity. Recover 1 willpower. Move any Wounds in your deck to your discard pile. Then reshuffle your deck without those discarded Wounds; they return on the next reshuffle.

**Long rest:** 8 hours of rest, of which at least 6 hours must be spent sleeping. Recover all willpower. Remove up to your Toughness in Wounds from your deck. Move any remaining Wounds in your deck to your discard pile. Then reshuffle your deck without those discarded Wounds; they return on the next reshuffle.

**Medical aid:** in a village or city can also remove Wounds, usually for a price, decided by your GM.

## Willpower

Each character has a **willpower** pool — typically **5 points** at full reserve. Willpower is a character resource, not a card.

You spend willpower to:

- Convert all Fate cards into Successes during a skill check or hit draw
- Activate certain class abilities, World Weavings, or items, per their rules

You recover willpower by:

- **Short rest** — recover 1 willpower
- **Long rest** — recover all willpower

The GM may also offer willpower recovery moments during dramatic beats — when you accomplish something heroic.


{{note
##### Tip
Don't hoard willpower. The system rewards using it. A held willpower at session's end is a turn you didn't claim a narration, an attack you didn't crit, a moment that didn't get to be yours.
}}

# The Lantern at Hollow Crossing

*A starter scenario for 3–5 players + 1 GM. Designed for one 2–3 hour session.*

Tevin the Lampwright has been missing for three days. His apprentice, Ailen, is offering coin to anyone who can find him. The party is on the road to the Hollow Crossing inn — and that road is where this story begins.

The party doesn't know what they're walking into. Neither do you. **That's the fun.**

{{note
##### GM Note — Pacing
This scenario has four acts. Each highlights a different part of the system: combat, social play, tense roleplay, and the narration mechanic. Pacing matters more than precision — if a scene drags, push forward. If players are loving a moment, give it air.

Total runtime target: 2–3 hours. If you're running long, the natural breakpoint is between Act 2 and Act 3 — pause the session at the inn and resume at the cave next time.
}}

## Cast of named characters

- **Tevin the Lampwright** — middle-aged, soft-spoken, knows more than he should. Currently bound in the Crystal Cave, awaiting "them."
- **Ailen** — Tevin's apprentice. Worried, exhausted, has already searched the road herself.
- **Edran** — retired dwarven miner, drinks in the inn most evenings. Knows about the Crystal Cave because Tevin asked him about it two weeks ago.
- **Olwin** — local trapper, will share what he saw "for a drink." Saw "shapes moving wrong" near the pines two nights ago.
- **The goblins** — six attack the party in Act 1; three more guard Tevin in Act 3. They were hired by *the Pale Hand* to retrieve the Lampwright "alive only."
- **The Two** — Travelers, beings of neutral alignment who pass between worlds. Not common knowledge. They arrive at the end of Act 3 and resolve Act 4.

# Act 1 — Roads and Goblins

*~35–45 min*

## Player intros

Before play, ask each player at the table:

- *Who is your character, in two sentences?*
- *Do any of you already know each other? How?*
- *Why are you on this road today?*

The pre-mades suggest backgrounds; let players fill in the rest. Their shared reason for traveling can be as simple as *"we hired the same caravan"* — don't overcomplicate it.

{{note
##### GM Note
Player intros set the table's social temperature. Be patient. If a player blanks, prompt them: *"Did you grow up nearby? In a city? In the wilds?"* No wrong answers.
}}

## The ambush

Read aloud, or paraphrase:

> *The trail winds through a band of dark pines, an hour out from the Hollow Crossing inn. The afternoon light is thin and cold. You hear nothing — no birds, no wind through the needles. Only the soft crunch of your own footsteps on frozen ground.*
>
> *Then arrows.*

Six goblins ambush from the treeline — three with shortbows on the rises, three with crude blades closing in.

**Goblins use the goblin deck**: control deck + 3 goblin-specific cards. See the printable NPC pack.

{{descriptive

This is the players' first combat. Don't go easy, but be ready to narrate goblin retreat if the party struggles. Goblins are skittish — drop one and the rest may hesitate. Lean into your party's once-per-long-rest abilities as natural showcases — most are designed to feel like a hero moment when they fire.
}}

## After the fight

Searching the bodies turns up a crude scrap of paper. A drawing of a lantern. A name — *Tevin*. And a clumsy promise of payment, signed with a single mark: a pale handprint.

If the party leaves a goblin alive and presses it for information, it'll talk.

{{descriptive
##### Tactical suggestion
Use this as the first skill check moment. A Social check, target 2, is the natural fit — the goblin is scared and pliable. If a player spends willpower for narration here, encourage them to introduce a small detail about the Pale Hand: a place name, a rumor, anything that hooks the table.

The goblin's confession: they were hired by *the Pale Hand* to bring the Lampwright in alive. The goblin doesn't know who that is — only the symbol. *"Pale Hand pays good. Was supposed to be easy."*

# Act 2 — The Hollow Crossing Inn

*~25–35 min*

The inn sits at a fork in the road. Two stories of dark timber and yellow window-light. Inside: a low common room, a fire, half a dozen locals nursing cups of mulled wine.

The mood is uneasy. Tevin's absence is the thing nobody is quite saying.

## What the locals share, with a little prompting

- Tevin left three days ago for a delivery to the next village. He never arrived.
- **Ailen**, his apprentice, has already searched the road herself. She is exhausted and quietly desperate. She offers a reward of 20 gp for Tevin's return.
- A trapper named **Olwin** saw *"shapes moving wrong"* near the pines two nights ago. He won't say more without a drink.
- An old dwarf — **Edran** — looks up from his cup when Tevin's name is mentioned. He needs persuasion to share, but eventually:

> *"He asked me about the Crystal Cave a fortnight back. Said he was hunting a light that can only be born there. Light from the world between worlds, he called it. I told him to leave it alone."*

## Skill checks here

Social, Alertness, and Intelligence checks fit naturally. **Edran is the most useful source** but needs to be coaxed — he thinks Tevin was meddling with things best left alone.

{{descriptive

# Act 3 — The Crystal Cave

*~30–40 min*

The cave mouth lies a half-day's hike into the hills above Hollow Crossing — a narrow split in the rock, half-hidden behind ferns. Inside, the passage opens into a cathedral of crystal: walls glittering with veins of clear stone that catch lantern-light and break it into hundreds of soft colors.

![crystal_cave](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/frankugh/weavers_of_power/main/images/player_handbook/crystal_cave.png) {width:320px}

In the center of the chamber, **Tevin sits bound** against a column of crystal. His face is bruised but composed. **Three goblins** crouch nearby — armed, watchful, visibly nervous. None of them are looking at Tevin. They're looking toward the cave entrance.

As the party approaches, Tevin's voice carries softly across the chamber:

> *"Untie me. Not for my sake — for yours. There isn't much time."*

The goblins hiss at him to be quiet, but they don't strike him.

## What the party can do

- **Fight.** The goblins will defend themselves but break easily. They want to live more than they want the prize.
- **Negotiate.** Insight (Social) checks reveal the goblins are out of their depth.
- **Sneak.** Stealth checks for surprise advantage if the party wants the upper hand without a clean fight.

{{note
##### GM Note — Letting the scene breathe
Players will probably expect combat. Let them surprise you. If they choose to talk, lean into it — the goblins have nothing left to gain by fighting. If a player asks Tevin what he means by *"there isn't much time,"* let him answer in fragments: *"They are coming. They were always going to come."*
}}

## The pivot

Whatever the party chooses, partway through the scene the goblins freeze. One whispers in a language the party doesn't know. Then they break and run — toward the deeper tunnels, away from the entrance. They don't look back.

Tevin closes his eyes. *"Ah. Well. Let them in, then."*

# Act 4 — The Travelers

*20–40 min*

The pivot in Act 3 has happened — the goblins fled toward the deeper tunnels, and now their captain bellows orders from the dark. They're not running anymore. They're regrouping.

A heavy growl echoes from those same tunnels.

## What comes from the dark

**Five goblins** rejoin the fight, stiffer-spined now under their captain's barking voice.

**Grizz the Skullbinder** — a goblin captain with a name and a presence. He commands the rest with cruel competence.

**Sindra** — a great wolf, a Warg, scarred and hungry. All teeth, no patience.

Tevin, still bound against the crystal column, looks at the goblins almost sympathetically. He speaks without raising his voice:

> *"You're standing on glass, friends. The longer you stay here, the more cracks I can hear."*

The goblins understand none of it, and they hate that they can't.

{{descriptive
##### Tevin's strange calm
Throughout this scene, Tevin remains bound — the goblins won't risk freeing him, and he won't ask. He continues to address them in quiet, businesslike tones, as if explaining to children the dire situation they've gotten themselves into.

Suggested lines, weave them in between rounds:
- *"They're not gods. They're not demons. They're just... travelers. And they really don't want to be here."*
- *"Look at the one you sent into the tunnel. Does he look brave to you, or just lost?"*
- *"You can still walk out, you know. Through that mouth. Right now."*

Tevin is the table's calmest character even as combat erupts around him. That's a feature.
}}

## Two paths to the Travelers

The party's first instinct shapes when the Travelers arrive.

### If the party engages the goblins themselves

(Lays a trap, charges in, picks off the captain first — anything proactive.)

After 1–2 rounds, the cave entrance brightens. Two pale figures step in. They watch for a moment, expressions of profound discomfort on their faces — something between disgust and grief. They will not fight.

After observing for one more round, they begin to emit a soft, monotone chant — and the party feels strength move through them. The **Pale Light Buff** activates at the start of their next turn.

### If the party hesitates

(Standing back. Counting goblins. Looking at each other with *"this is a lot."*)

The cave entrance brightens. Two pale figures step in — too quiet, too still. They do not look at the goblins. They look at the party. One of them speaks, and both their voices speak as one:

> *"We would ask a service of you. The work below is unclean, and our hands are not made for it. Help us, and you will feel the light we carry."*

They extend a hand. The party feels a pulse of warmth — a taste of what is offered. Then the offer pauses, awaiting their answer.

{{note
##### The Pale Light Buff
*Granted as the Travelers begin their monotone chant.*

**Pre-combat (immediate):**
- All willpower restored to maximum
- +1 effective Power for 10 minutes (one extra card per Draw of Power)

**During combat — each Traveler may, instead of drawing for themselves, channel one of these to a single PC:**
- **Granted Action** — that PC gets +1 action on their next turn
- **Restoration** — that PC refills Toughness to max and recovers 1 willpower
- **Channeled Light** — that PC may change the energy type of one card in their hand to any energy type

**Once per fight, both Travelers together:**
- **Choral Hymn** — both Travelers stand still and harmonize their chant. Every PC gains +1 hit card on attacks for one round.
}}

\page

## If the party accepts the offer (or has already engaged)

Combat begins. The Travelers will not attack — they protect themselves with reflective veils, healing energies, and brief gestures that send goblins stumbling backward. Their faces show distaste throughout the entire fight, even when they're helping. They prefer Pale Channeling over almost any other action.

## If the party refuses

The Travelers' faces tighten. They incline their heads, just barely.

> *"As you wish."*

They engage the goblins themselves. Mechanically, they are powerful — but they fight reluctantly, defensively, and take twice as long as they should to do anything. Their hesitation is genuine. Watching them is uncomfortable.

### The lantern threat

After 1–2 rounds, **Grizz the Skullbinder** notices something: the Travelers seem to draw strength from the lantern beside Tevin. He bellows in goblin-speak. Two goblins detach from the fight and run toward the lantern.

The Travelers look at the party again. Their voices now carry an edge they did not have before — something close to quiet panic, beneath the formal phrasing:

> *"We ask you again. The lantern must not break. Stand with us."*

{{note
##### GM Note — the second offer
This is the players' last chance to step in. If they refuse or hesitate too long, narrate the consequence below — don't soften it. The lesson the scene teaches is that the Travelers' help wasn't optional flavor; it was the world asking for theirs.
}}

If the party still refuses or hesitates too long: the goblins reach the lantern. They raise crude blades. They swing.

## The lantern shatters

Light explodes outward — pale, cold, painfully bright. The goblins scream, high and wrong, and crumple to the cave floor. Their bodies are unmarked, but they are not breathing.

The Travelers stand over Tevin, sheltering him from the blast with their own bodies. When the light fades, they are still there. Tevin is unhurt. The goblins are dead.

The Travelers do not look at the party. One of them speaks, softly:

> *"The world has lost an artifact of protection today. The dark one is one step closer to what he wants. We will remember this."*

They take Tevin's bonds in hand and they fall away. They lift Tevin himself — gently — and turn toward the cave entrance. They do not look back.

The lantern is shards on the cave floor.

## If the party helped

After the goblins are dead, the Travelers turn to the party. Their faces relax — just slightly. The discomfort doesn't leave entirely, but gratitude is there, somewhere behind their composure.

> *"You did what we could not. You did it cleanly enough, and we are in your debt. The light remembers those who help it return."*

They take the lantern and lift Tevin's bonds away. Before they leave, one of them looks at the party — really looks, for the first time:

> *"You did not need to be here. That you were is remembered."*

Then they are gone — through the cave mouth, into a light that should not be there. Tevin watches them leave.

> *"They'll bring it home. That's all that matters now. Thank you."*

## Closing the session

**If the lantern was saved:** Tevin travels back to Hollow Crossing with the party. Ailen weeps and laughs and hugs him. Edran, in the inn that night, says nothing — but raises his cup when Tevin walks in. The Pale Hand is still out there. They wanted the lantern. They didn't get it.

**If the lantern was lost:** Tevin returns to Hollow Crossing alone — the party walking with him in heavier silence than they came in. He is grateful but quieter. The villagers don't understand what was lost. That evening, the wind shifts.

# Reference Card

## Core draws

**Skill check:** draw cards = ability score. ✓ Success / ◇ Fate / ✗ Fail. Spend 1 willpower → all Fates become Successes.

**Hit draw:** draw 3 cards. 1+ hit, 2+ bonus, 3 critical. Wounds = Fail. Spend willpower as above.

**Draw of Power:** draw to your effective Power level. Wounds in hand count.

## Turn

**Turn:** movement + 2 actions + free actions. End of turn: discard non-Wound cards.

**Shed:** non-energy action. Discard 1 Wound card from your hand.

**Prepare:** non-energy action. Draw 1 more card on your next Draw of Power. You can never gain more than +3 extra draw from Prepare, Strengthen, or any other source combined.

**Opportunity attack:** leaving an enemy's melee reach without Disengage lets that enemy make one basic melee attack. Max once per creature per round.

## Damage and recovery

**Damage:** Armor → Guard → Toughness. At Toughness 0: take 1 Wound, reset Toughness, then apply any excess damage.

**Willpower:** max 5. Recover 1 on short rest. Recover all on long rest.

**Short rest:** about 1 hour of downtime activity. Move any Wounds in your deck to discard, then reshuffle without them. They return on the next reshuffle.

**Long rest:** 8 hours of rest, at least 6 spent sleeping. Remove Wounds from your deck up to your Toughness. Move remaining Wounds in your deck to discard, then reshuffle without them. They return on the next reshuffle.

**Advantage:** +1 hit card. **Disadvantage:** −1 hit card. Doesn't stack.

**World Weavings:** out-of-combat abilities on your sheet. **Threads** are free unless noted. Each **Pattern** can be used once per rest. Patterns usually take a minute or more to weave, though some Martial Patterns happen in the moment. Don't use actions or energy unless noted.

Thank you for playing the Weavers of Power v0.5 quickstart. Your feedback shapes the next version.
